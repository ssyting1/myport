My Product Philosophy

I don't build features. I improve decisions.

AI is powerful. The real work is deciding where it belongs.

I build products for real-world chaos. I started in logistics SaaS — which is a polite way of saying I worked in environments where something is always late, someone is always calling, and something is always on fire.

My early job was simple: talk to customers, untangle ugly workflows, and ship things that make dispatchers breathe easier. Most of the value came down to three questions: Where is it? When will it get there? And why is this one different?

Over time, that became helping systems learn from the mess.

I think about AI in practical terms:

What decision are we improving?
What happens when the model is wrong?
Can the user understand and trust the output?

If a simple rule solves it, use the rule.

If the data is weak, fix the data.

If the decision is high impact and patterns exist — that's where AI belongs.

In operations-heavy environments, data, UX, and models are one system. If inputs are noisy, predictions get expensive. Bad ETAs become overtime. Overtime becomes churn.

So I spend time in the less glamorous layers — data contracts, edge cases, failure modes. Because a polished AI feature built on shaky inputs is just a confident mistake.

When I design AI features, I focus on:

  • Decision support before full automation
  • Clear signals over black boxes
  • Visible confidence levels
  • Human override by default
  • Measurable outcomes, not model accuracy theater

The goal isn't to replace the operator. It's to give them leverage.
